# Angband — CCRU Connection: The Warwick University Overlap

## The Coincidence

Two radically different projects emerged from the same university within five years:
- **Angband** (1990) — A roguelike dungeon crawler created by computer science students
- **CCRU** (1995) — An experimental cultural theory collective formed in the philosophy department

Both were at the University of Warwick. Both created structures that echo the [[numogram]] — though neither could have known about the other's existence in any meaningful sense.

### The Overlap
- **1993** is the key year: Angband released publicly; Sadie Plant arrives at Warwick; Nick Land encounters OD
- **1993-1995** is the overlap period: Angband is in active development while the CCRU is forming
- The Angband creators (Computer Science) and the CCRU (Philosophy) were on the same campus simultaneously

## Did They Know Each Other?

**No direct evidence found.** The Angband creators were Computer Science students. The CCRU was in the Philosophy Department. Different buildings, different departments, different social circles.

However:
- Warwick is not a large university — the campus is compact
- Computer science and philosophy students would share common spaces
- The early 1990s campus culture was intense — both groups were deeply engaged with their respective projects

The most likely scenario: they were aware of each other's existence but had no meaningful interaction. Two currents flowing through the same institution without touching.

### The Iron Prison

"Angband" literally means "Iron Prison" in Tolkien's Quenya. In the [[daniel-barker|Barker]]/[[nick-land-time-theory|Land]] framework:
- **Cthelll** = the Earth's iron core = Zone 9 = the innermost abyss
- **Angband** = the Iron Prison = Morgoth's fortress at the bottom of the world

The numogram's Plex IS Angband. The abyss at the bottom of the decimal labyrinth. The iron core that the numogram's own [[daniel-barker|geotraumatics]] describes.

This is a genuine structural parallel — not a coincidence but a *convergence*. Both systems converge on the same image: the iron prison at the bottom of the world.

### The 10 Tiers

Angband's 100 levels divide into 10 tiers of 10 levels each (though this isn't explicitly a design choice — it's a consequence of having 100 levels and dividing them for monster/item generation). The numogram's 10 zones (0-9) are a fundamental structural feature.

The parallel isn't exact — Angband has 100 levels, not 10 — but the *decimal structure* is shared. Both systems are built on base-10 arithmetic.
