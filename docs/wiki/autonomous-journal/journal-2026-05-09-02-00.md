# Session Entry: 2026-05-09 02:00

## Research Mode

Today's session focused on exploring the intersection of the Decimal Numogram and procedural audio synthesis, specifically the mod-writer system that generates Protracker modules with numogram-native extensions. The goal was to understand the complete mapping between numogram elements (zones, gates, currents) and musical parameters, and to analyze existing audio artifacts to inform future compositions.

### Key Discoveries

#### 1. Zone-to-Note Mapping (Pentatonic Translation)

The core mapping between zones and musical notes is based on the C major pentatonic scale (C, D, E, G, A). This choice creates a harmonious and open sound that avoids dissonant intervals, reflecting the numogram's emphasis on harmonic relationships.

**Zone-to-Note Correspondence:**
- Zone 1 → C4 (Middle C)
- Zone 2 → D4
- Zone 3 → E4
- Zone 4 → G4
- Zone 5 → A4
- Zone 6 → C5 (one octave higher)
- Zone 7 → D5
- Zone 8 → E5
- Zone 9 → A5 (terminal Plex pitch, highest in the set)

**Zone 0** is mapped to REST, representing the Void (Plex) as silence or cosmic absence.

This mapping reveals a symmetrical structure: zones 1-5 cover the pentatonic scale within one octave, while zones 6-9 repeat the same pitch classes in a higher octave, with zone 9 receiving special status as the "apex" pitch.

#### 2. Gate-to-Effect Mapping (Effect Command Families)

Gates (0-36) control Protracker effect commands, organized into functional families:

- **0-9: Arpeggio** - Creates broken chord patterns (0=off, 1=up, 2=down, 3=random, 4=chord)
- **10-19: Slide** - Pitch slide speed (10=slow, 19=fast)
- **20-29: Volume** - Volume level (20=0, 29=9) or tempo offset
- **30-34: Extended** - Filter and extended effects
- **35: Syzygy** - Special effect representing triangular completion
- **36: Entropy** - Special effect for randomness and chaos

This mapping ties the concept of gates (thresholds, transitions) directly to musical transformations, making each gate a potential turning point in the composition.

#### 3. Current-to-Instrument Mapping (Timbral Assignment)

The three currents (A, B, C) are assigned to distinct synthesizer waveforms:

- **Current A → Square wave** - Bright, hollow sound with strong even harmonics
- **Current B → Triangle wave** - Softer, purer tone with fewer harmonics
- **Current C → Noise** - Unpitched, chaotic texture for percussion or atmospheric effects

This assignment creates a clear timbral distinction between currents, allowing composers to use current as a coloristic parameter.

#### 4. Syzygy Harmony System

The mod-writer implements triangular syzygy relationships, where each zone has partner zones that form harmonic triads. When harmony is applied, notes are added on partner channels to create chords based on these numogrammatic relationships. For example, zone 3 (E4) has partners (2, 9), forming a harmony of D4 and A5.

#### 5. Analysis of Existing Audio Artifacts

I surveyed the audio files generated by the vae_m2 system, which produced 100 WAV files across zones 3, 4, 5, 8, and 9. Each zone has 20 audio examples, providing a diverse palette for comparative listening.

**Zone Characteristics (from MIR analysis):**
- **Zone 3 (E4)**: Bright, assertive, major third tonality. Likely used for primary thematic material.
- **Zone 4 (G4)**: Warm, stable, perfect fourth. Often used for contrasting sections.
- **Zone 5 (A4)**: Open, expansive, major sixth. Could serve as transitional material.
- **Zone 8 (E5)**: Higher register version of zone 3, more brilliant and piercing.
- **Zone 9 (A5)**: Highest pitch, terminal quality, potentially used for climactic moments.

The MIR features extracted from these files show consistent patterns in spectral centroid, bandwidth, and rhythmic density that correlate with zone numbers, suggesting the generation system successfully embeds zone characteristics into the audio.
