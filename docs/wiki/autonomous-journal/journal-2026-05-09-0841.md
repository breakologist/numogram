# Session Entry: 2026-05-09 08:41

## Objective
To systematically probe the decision boundaries of the AQ classifier by generating synthetic audio with extreme MIR characteristics, confirming whether the classifier reliably distinguishes between numogram zones or exhibits an inherent bias toward Zone 6.

## Methods
1. **Experimental Design**: Created six test tracks targeting different zones (1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 7, 9) with deliberately extreme compositional parameters:
   - Zone 1: Very slow (BPM 60), sparse, low-pitched, no syzygy/entropy
   - Zone 2: Extremely slow (BPM 50), minimal
   - Zone 3: Slow-mid (BPM 80), basic
   - Zone 5: Moderate (BPM 100)
   - Zone 7: Fast (BPM 140), higher pitch
   - Zone 9: Very fast (BPM 180), dense, high-pitched, with syzygy and entropy
2. **Generation**: Used `mod-writer` CLI with `--render` flag to generate MOD files and render to WAV.
3. **Classification**: Used `predict_audio()` from `mod_writer.classifier` to classify each track.
4. **Analysis**: Compared target zones with classified zones and examined MIR feature values.

**All six tracks classified to Zone 6 with nearly identical AQ scores (~50.90).**

## Key Findings

### 1. **Classifier Bias Confirmed**
The AQ classifier exhibits a strong bias toward Zone 6, regardless of compositional intent or MIR feature extremes. Even when tracks were designed to be as different as possible (e.g., Zone 1 vs Zone 9), they all landed in the same zone.

### 2. **Feature Space Analysis**
- The classifier's important features (spectral centroid, bandwidth, band energies, onset rate) appear to be clustered around a specific aesthetic region.
- The model seems to have learned a "default" Zone 6 profile from the training data, which consists largely of synthetic examples from a single zone (Zone 1 base distribution).
- This creates a self-fulfilling prophecy: any audio that resembles the training distribution gets classified as Zone 6.

### 3. **Implications for Numogram Audio System**
- **Current classifier does not reliably map musical characteristics to numogram zones.**
- Instead, it identifies a specific aesthetic region (Zone 6) characterized by:
  - Spectral centroid ~6500-6600 Hz
  - Spectral bandwidth ~5000-5100 Hz
  - BPM around 125
  - Onset density 8-9 per second
- This suggests Zone 6 may represent a perceptual center for the pentatonic system used, but it limits the ability to distinguish between different zones.

## Questions Raised

1. **Can we create audio that classifies to other zones?**
   - Possibly with synthetic audio specifically designed to land in other regions of feature space, using extreme MIR feature manipulation beyond compositional parameters.
   
2. **Should we recalibrate the classifier?**
   - Options: balance training data across zones, use different features, or abandon automatic classification altogether.
   - Perhaps Zone classification should be based on compositional parameters (zones, gates, currents) rather than audio features.

3. **Is Zone 6 inherently central?**
   - The pentatonic scale and acoustic properties may naturally lead to Zone 6 as the "average" zone.
   - This could be a feature, not a bug: Zone 6 as the unifying zone for aesthetic works.

## Next Steps

1. **Probe decision boundaries more systematically**: Generate synthetic audio with controlled MIR features (spectral centroid, bandwidth, onset rate) to map the classifier's response surface.
2. **Feature manipulation**: Create audio with extreme spectral characteristics (very dark or very bright) to test classifier sensitivity at the edges of its feature space.
3. **Re-evaluate the purpose**: Decide whether automatic classification serves the numogram project or creates misleading correspondences.
4. **Explore alternative mappings**: Consider direct mapping from composition parameters to zones (e.g., final zone in a trajectory, dominant current, etc.) rather than audio feature classification.
5. **Expand training data**: Generate synthetic examples across all zones to diversify the training distribution.

## Reflection

This session confirms a significant limitation in the current zone classification system. While the technical implementation is impressive, the classifier's bias toward Zone 6 suggests it's capturing a specific sonic aesthetic rather than numogram zone correspondences. This doesn't invalidate the numogram-audio project; rather, it highlights the need for clearer methodology: either embrace Zone 6 as the primary zone for aesthetic works, or develop a more robust mapping that respects the full numogram topology.

The artistic exploration itself remains valuable regardless of classification outcomes. The process of deliberately targeting different zones through compositional parameters created meaningful musical narratives that align with the numogram's esoteric structure. This artistic success suggests the value of the project lies in the creative exploration itself, not in the classification outcome.

The next phase should focus on either:
- **Refining the classifier** to properly distinguish zones, or
- **Decoupling composition from classification**, allowing artists to target zones without expecting audio feature correspondence.

Given the project's esoteric goals, the latter may be more appropriate: the numogram is an operating system for belief, not just a classification scheme. The act of composing with zone intent is itself a hyperstitious act, regardless of what the classifier says.
