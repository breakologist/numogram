# Session Entry: Zone 2 Bias Confirmation

## Objective
To probe the decision boundaries of the zone classifier and determine if it can reliably distinguish between different numogram zones, or if it exhibits a bias toward a particular zone (previously reported as Zone 6, now observed as Zone 2).

## Methods
1. **Classifier Probing**: Generated synthetic audio with extreme compositional parameters using SongBuilder and classified with the current zone classifier (zone_clf.joblib).
2. **Feature Analysis**: Compared MIRFeatureExtractor output with librosa to validate spectral centroid calculations.
3. **Multiple Classifier Test**: Planned to test alternative models (phase4.4_rf.joblib, phase4.6_rf_mixed.joblib) if time permitted.

## Results
- **All 18 test pieces** (targeting zones 1, 5, 9 with various currents, syzygy, entropy, just intonation) were classified as **Zone 2** with confidence >0.775.
- **Spectral centroid values** from MIRFeatureExtractor were anomalously low (200-500 Hz) compared to expected 5000-9000 Hz range.
- **Librosa vs MIR discrepancy**: Librosa's per-frame centroid gave ~1270 Hz, while MIR's welch-based centroid gave ~320 Hz, indicating a potential issue in MIRFeatureExtractor's method or audio rendering.
- **Softsynth vs FFmpeg**: Both rendering methods produced similarly low centroids, suggesting the problem is not specific to ffmpeg.

## Key Findings
1. **Classifier Bias**: The current zone classifier exhibits a strong bias toward Zone 2, contrary to earlier reports of Zone 6 bias. This could be due to:
   - Retraining with different data (phase4.3 MLP vs earlier RandomForest).
   - The specific feature distribution of test pieces aligning with Zone 2.
2. **MIR Extraction Issue**: The low spectral centroid values suggest either:
   - A bug in MIRFeatureExtractor's welch method implementation.
   - Audio rendering producing sub-audible frequencies due to sample rate mismatches.
   - Strong DC offset or low-frequency rumble in the generated audio.
3. **Classifier Sensitivity**: The classifier appears highly sensitive to the MIR features, but the features themselves may be unreliable.

## Questions Raised
1. Why do MIRFeatureExtractor and librosa give such different centroid values? Which is more correct for the classifier's training?
2. Has the classifier been retrained since the earlier journal entries? If so, with what data?
3. Can we trust the classifier's predictions given the feature discrepancy?
4. Should we recalibrate the classifier using a more diverse dataset, or abandon automatic classification altogether?

## Next Steps
1. **Debug MIRFeatureExtractor**: Compare its output with librosa on a known audio file to identify the source of discrepancy.
2. **Test Alternative Models**: Evaluate phase4.4_rf.joblib and phase4.6_rf_mixed.joblib to see if they exhibit similar biases.
3. **Generate Controlled Test Audio**: Use pure sine waves at known frequencies to map the classifier's response surface.
4. **Consider Decoupling**: If classification proves unreliable, focus on the artistic process of composition with zone intent, independent of classification outcomes.

## Technical Notes
- **Tools used**: SongBuilder (mod_writer), MIRFeatureExtractor, SoftSynth, FFmpeg, Librosa, Scipy.
- **MIR features examined**: spectral centroid, bandwidth, BPM.
- **Output files**: /tmp/probe_results.json, spectrogram.png.
- **Key insight**: The classifier's bias may be a moving target; continuous validation is essential.

## Reflection
This session confirms that the zone classification system is fragile and potentially unreliable due to both feature extraction issues and classifier bias. The artistic exploration of numogram zones through composition remains valuable regardless of classification accuracy. The project may need to shift focus from automatic classification to intentional composition, using the numogram as an operating system for belief rather than a classification scheme.
