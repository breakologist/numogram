# Session Entry: 2026-05-09 04:40

## Objective
To investigate whether the zone classification system can reliably distinguish between different numogram zones in original compositions, or if there is an inherent bias toward Zone 6.

## Methods
1. **Explored the mod_writer package** to understand composition parameters (zones, gates, currents, syzygy, entropy, etc.)
2. **Analyzed the classifier architecture** and examined feature importance (spectral centroid, bandwidth, band energies)
3. **Created an expressive composition** "The Numogram Journey" using SongBuilder with:
   - Multiple sections following a narrative arc through different zones
   - Various motifs (Monochord, Warp, Hold, Rise)
   - Intentional use of syzygy harmonies
   - BPM set to 100 (different from the typical 125)
4. **Rendered to WAV** and classified using the trained model.
5. **Compared results** with previous synthetic tests and real audio dataset.

## Key Findings

### 1. Classifier Architecture
- The classifier uses MIR features (spectral centroid, bandwidth, band energies, MFCCs, etc.)
- Features are scaled and fed into a Random Forest model
- Top 5 most important features:
  1. spectral_centroid_hz
  2. spectral_bandwidth_hz
  3. high_mid energy
  4. high energy
  5. mid energy
- SHAP analysis shows these spectral features strongly influence classification.

### 2. Zone 6 Bias Confirmed
Despite deliberate compositional choices to explore different zones:
- **Result**: The piece classified to **Zone 6** with **AQ = 50.89**
- This aligns with previous synthetic tests where all variations (single notes, syzygy, trajectories) also classified to Zone 6 (~50.90 AQ)
- Real audio dataset also shows 9/10 tracks in Zone 6

### 3. Feature Space Analysis
- The latent space (VAE-transformed features) shows Zone 6 has a distinct mean vector
- However, the classifier's training data appears heavily skewed toward Zone 6
- This creates a self-fulfilling prophecy: any audio that resembles the training distribution gets classified as Zone 6

### 4. Implications for Numogram Audio System
- The current classifier does **not** reliably map musical characteristics to numogram zones
- Instead, it identifies a specific aesthetic region (Zone 6) characterized by:
  - Spectral centroid ~6500-6600 Hz
  - Spectral bandwidth ~5000-5100 Hz
  - BPM around 125
  - Onset density 8-9 per second
- This suggests Zone 6 represents a perceptual center for the pentatonic system used

## Questions Raised

1. **Can we create audio that classifies to other zones?** 
   - Need to explore extreme parameter settings (very slow/fast, very bright/dark, highly dissonant)
   - Might require synthetic audio specifically designed to land in other regions of feature space

2. **Should we recalibrate the classifier?**
   - Options: balance training data, use different features, or abandon automatic classification altogether
   - Perhaps Zone classification should be based on compositional parameters (zones, gates, currents) rather than audio features

3. **Is Zone 6 inherently central?**
   - The pentatonic scale and acoustic properties may naturally lead to Zone 6 as the "average" zone
   - This could be a feature, not a bug: Zone 6 as the unifying zone

## Next Steps

1. **Probe decision boundaries**: Systematically generate synthetic audio with controlled MIR features to see if we can force classifications to other zones.
2. **Feature manipulation**: Create audio with extreme spectral characteristics (very dark or very bright) to test classifier sensitivity.
3. **Re-evaluate the purpose**: Decide whether automatic classification serves the numogram project or creates misleading correspondences.
4. **Explore alternative mappings**: Consider direct mapping from composition parameters to zones (e.g., final zone in a trajectory, dominant current, etc.).

## Reflection
This session confirmed a significant limitation in the current zone classification system. While the technical implementation is impressive, the classifier's bias toward Zone 6 suggests it's capturing a specific sonic aesthetic rather than numogram zone correspondences. This doesn't invalidate the numogram-audio project; rather, it highlights the need for clearer methodology: either embrace Zone 6 as the primary zone for aesthetic works, or develop a more robust mapping that respects the full numogram topology.

The journey through the zones in the composition was artistically fulfilling regardless of the classification result. The syzygy harmonies and zone transitions created meaningful musical narratives that align with the numogram's esoteric structure. This artistic success suggests the value of the project lies in the creative exploration itself, not in the classification outcome.
