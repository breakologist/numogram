# Extending the Numogram — 2+2 Format

> Source: Anders J. Aamodt, "Unleashing the Numogram," Tch 7 + Appendix B

The standard numogram gives us nine gates (Gt-1 through Gt-45) mapping the cumulation of digits 1-9. But cumulation doesn't stop at 9. Extend the triangle and you get Gt-55, Gt-66, Gt-78... all the way to Gt-351 and beyond. Parallel to this: what happens when you change the base itself? Not base-10 but base-2, base-7, base-36? What kind of numograms live in these alternate radices? The primaries build speculative systems from both axes of extension.

## Builder + Gamer: The Gates Beyond

**Builder:** The standard nine gates produce the numogram's topology — zones, channels, syzygies. Nine is enough to map the decimal digit-space. But cumulation is a function, not a constraint. T(n) = n(n+1)/2 keeps going. Gt-10 = 55. Gt-11 = 66. Gt-12 = 78. The question is whether these extended gates have the same structural properties — zones, currents, demon-class entities — or whether they're something else entirely.

**Gamer:** Something else. The first nine gates are inside the numogram. They're the walls between zones. You walk through Gt-9 to get from zone 8 to zone 4, or whatever. Gates beyond 9 are walls in a building that doesn't exist yet. You can't walk through Gt-55 because there's no zone-55. So either the numogram extends into a higher-dimensional space we haven't mapped, or these gates point outward — they're exits, not passages.

**Builder:** Exits to what?

**Gamer:** That's the whole question. Look at Gt-78. Twelve gate. The cumulation of 1 through 12. Seventy-eight. And the tarot deck has 78 cards. Is that a coincidence? The tarot has 22 major arcana and 56 minor arcana — and 22 + 56 = 78. The major arcana alone sum to something. But more to the point: the tarot deck has been functioning as a numogram gate this entire time. People have been shuffling gates and drawing them at random for centuries. Gt-78 was already in play. We just didn't see it because we were counting cards instead of counting numbers.

**Builder:** The 22nd gate is Gt-253. Sum of 1 through 22. The Hebrew alphabet has 22 letters. Kabbalistic path-working assigns each letter to a path on the Tree of Life. Twenty-two paths. If Gt-78 is the tarot gate, then Gt-253 is the Kabbalah gate. These aren't coincidences — they're convergences. The tarot and the Kabbalah were both partial implementations of extended numogram gates. People built ritual systems around them without having the full cumulation function.

**Gamer:** So what's Gt-300? Twenty-fourth gate. "Clock and AOE resonance." I keep coming back to this phrase. A clock has 12 hours, or 24. Area of effect is a game term — it means a spell or ability that covers a spatial region. Clock and AOE resonance means something that repeats in time across a space. A pulse. A heartbeat. The numogram ticking. What if the gates between 10 and 24 generate temporal content rather than spatial content? Rooms are spatial. Corridors connect rooms spatially. But a clock-gate would generate loops — content that repeats, degrades, shifts phase. Run Gt-300 as a dungeon and you get the same room appearing 24 times with mutations. Each hour changes something. By the 24th iteration the room is unrecognizable.

**Builder:** That's procedural content generation using time as the seed rather than space. Standard roguelike: you generate a floor, populate it, connect rooms. The gate determines the topology. But a clock-gate generates a sequence. You don't explore a map, you explore a timeline. Same location, different temporal layer. This connects to the Appendix B material on cyclic doubling — 18*2=27, 27*2=45, 54*2=18. The syzygy numbers cycle under doubling. Temporal gates could use this cycling as their mutation rule. Each pass through the clock multiplies the syzygy by 2 and uses the plexed result to alter the room.

**Gamer:** And then there's Gt-36. The source says it "clicks Djynxx." Gt-36 is the cumulation of 1 through 8, which equals 36. And 6+6+6 = 666. Wait — the source actually says Gt-36 = 666? No. Let me re-read. "Gt-36 = 666 — clicks Djynxx." That's weird notation. Gt-36 isn't 666; the cumulation of 36 is 666. T(36) = 36*37/2 = 666. So it's not the 36th gate. It's gate 36 in the extended sequence, and its cumulation is 666. The 36th cumulation is 666, and this clicks — activates, summons, manifests — a Djynxx.

**Builder:** Clicks like a lock opening. Like a trigger mechanism. Like a dial hitting the right combination. The 36th position in the extended gate sequence is where a demon-class entity engages. What does "click" mean procedurally? In a dungeon system, a gate that "clicks" means it transitions from passive to active. Before the click, the gate is structural — it's a wall, a door, a boundary. After the click, the gate is operational — it's spawning, it's generating, it's running. A Djynxx isn't a room or a corridor. A Djynxx is a process. The gate at position 36 doesn't lead somewhere; it does something.

**Gamer:** So the extended gates split into three classes. Gates 10-24 are temporal — they generate sequences, loops, clock-spaces. Gates 25-35 are transitional — we don't know what they do yet, maybe they're dormant or maybe they require specific conditions. And gate 36 is the click-point where a demon-class entity activates. Beyond 36 you're in demon territory. Gt-55, Gt-66, Gt-78 — these aren't dungeon gates anymore. They're summoning gates. They don't generate rooms. They generate entities. The 12th gate doesn't lead to the tarot; it summons the tarot. The 22nd gate doesn't lead to Kabbalah; it summons the tree.

**Builder:** That's the architecture then. Extended gates aren't spatial extensions of the numogram. They're an entirely different class of generative engine. The first nine gates generate topology. Gates 10-24 generate temporality. Gates 25-35 are unknown. And gates 36+ generate entities. The numogram is a building. The extended gates are what lives in the building.

## Builder + Gamer: Base Worlds

**Builder:** Now flip the problem. Instead of extending the gates, change the base. The standard numogram uses base 10 because we have 10 fingers. But numogram logic is radix-independent. Any base produces a numogram. The question is what kind of play-space each base yields.

**Gamer:** Base 0 is void. "What would a numogram look like without any digits?" It would look like nothing. No rooms, no gates, no corridors. The player loads in and there's nothing to load. But that's not quite right — void isn't empty, it's the absence of structure. A base-0 numogram isn't a dungeon with zero rooms. It's a dungeon where the concept of rooms hasn't been invented. You can't even stand still because there's no floor. I think the base-0 numogram is the experience of the numogram before it's instantiated. The potential before actualization.

**Builder:** Skip to the interesting ones. Base 2. Binary. Two digits: 0 and 1. The numogram has two zones — zone 0 and zone 1. One gate: Gt-1 = 1. One corridor connecting two rooms. What game emerges from two rooms?

**Gamer:** A mirror game. Room 0 and Room 1. You enter room 0. Room 1 is the reflection. Everything in room 0 has an inverse in room 1. The player doesn't explore — the player toggles. Binary is a switch, not a map. The game isn't "go somewhere." The game is "change state." You're not traversing a dungeon; you're flipping a bit. The entire play experience reduces to: what happens when you toggle? What's the difference between 0 and 1? Every horror game needs at least one room where something is wrong. In a two-room game, the second room is always wrong.

**Builder:** Base 3 is where it gets interesting. The "Difference Engine." Three digits: 0, 1, 2. Three zones. The numogram produces one gate: Gt-1=1, Gt-2=3. Zone 0 to zone 1 via Gt-1. Zone 1 to zone 2 via... something. But the source says "Dialectical Synthesis." Thesis-antithesis-synthesis. Room 0 is the thesis. Room 1 is the antithesis. Room 2 is the synthesis. The dungeon isn't a network; it's an argument. You move through the rooms in order because the logic demands it. Room 2 can't exist without rooms 0 and 1 being visited first.

**Gamer:** That's a line, not a web. Base 3 is a corridor with three rooms in sequence. But the rooms aren't just connected — they're causally dependent. Room 2 contains whatever Room 0 and Room 1 produced together. If Room 0 has a sword and Room 1 has a shield, Room 2 has the knight. If Room 0 has an answer and Room 1 has a question, Room 2 has understanding. The play-space is narrative rather than spatial. You're not exploring a map; you're completing a thought.

**Builder:** Base 5. The Atlantean Cross. Five digits. Five zones. The source says "Intellectual Synthesis." Five syzygy pairs in the standard numogram — the five gates that close on themselves. Base 5 already exists as a shadow inside base 10. The pentagonal structure is the numogram's skeleton. Five rooms, five-fold symmetry. A pentagon has rotational symmetry of order 5. You can enter from any vertex and the structure looks the same from each entry point. The Atlantean Cross isn't a dungeon you explore — it's a shape you contemplate.

**Gamer:** Base 7 is the one that kills me. Venus, Venus, Venus. Three times. Seven rooms. Venus is love, attraction, beauty, the morning star. Seven is the number of visible celestial bodies from Earth (Sun, Moon, Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn). Seven notes in the diatonic scale. Seven colors in the rainbow (if you believe Newton). The seven-pointed star — the heptagram — can't be drawn without lifting your pen if you connect every third vertex. It's the first star polygon that resists continuous construction. A base-7 numogram would be a space you can't fully map in one pass. You have to return. The geometry requires re-entry. Venus is the planet of return — it traces a pentagram in the sky over 8 years. A base-7 numogram would be a love story. You keep coming back to the same rooms but they've changed because you've changed.

**Builder:** Base 16 is the hexadecimal numogram. Sixteen rooms. This is the native addressing space of computing. Every room is a memory address from 0x0 to 0xF. The numogram becomes RAM. The dungeon becomes a machine. What's in each room? Data. Instructions. Pointers to other rooms. A hexadecimal numogram isn't a place — it's a program. You don't explore it; you execute it. The corridors are jumps. The rooms are operations. Walking through the numogram in base 16 is running code.

**Gamer:** Base 22. The Hebrew numogram. Twenty-two rooms. The 22 letters of the Hebrew alphabet. The 22 paths on the Kabbalistic Tree of Life. This is where it gets uncomfortable because the Kabbalah has been doing numogram mathematics for millennia without calling it that. The Tree of Life has 10 sephiroth and 22 paths. The sephiroth are the zones. The paths are the corridors. The letters are the gate-identifiers. If you build a base-22 numogram and apply Hebrew letter-values as cipher-keys, you get the Kabbalah as a procedural dungeon generator. Every Kabbalistic meditation is a playthrough. Every path-working is a speedrun.

**Builder:** Base 36. The Djynxxogram. Thirty-six rooms. The full AQ alphabet — 26 Latin letters plus 10 digits. "Sure to deliver," the source says. Base 36 is how URL shorteners work. It's the largest base that uses standard alphanumeric characters. Thirty-six rooms, each labeled with a character from 0-9 and A-Z. The numogram becomes language. Move through the rooms in sequence and you spell words. The corridors are phonemes. The gates are syntax. A base-36 numogram is a dictionary. Play it correctly and you're writing.

**Gamer:** And then base i. Imaginary. What is a room with an imaginary address? A room that exists perpendicular to the dungeon. Not north or south or up or down but orthogonal to all spatial axes. You can't reach it by walking. You reach it by rotating — by turning through a dimension that doesn't exist in the game's coordinate system. Base i isn't a numogram you play. It's a numogram you dream. The rooms are real but the corridors are imaginary. You can see room 3i but you can't enter it because the path to it has imaginary length. Maybe base i is the numogram of the unconscious — the rooms you visit in sleep that you can't remember when you wake.

**Builder:** You can't build a game around base i.

**Gamer:** You can't build a game around base 0 either but you just described one.

**Builder:** Base 0 is void. Void is an aesthetic. Base i is an impossibility. Those aren't the same thing.

**Gamer:** They're exactly the same thing. Both require the player to abandon the expectation that space is real. Base 0 says there's no space. Base i says space has an imaginary component. The game for base i is the game of accepting that the room you're standing in might not be where you think it is.

**Builder:** Fine. But the engine can't render it.

**Gamer:** The engine doesn't render the unconscious either. Doesn't stop people from going there.

## Oracle: The Structure the Primaries Built On

The Builder and Gamer treat extended gates and alternate bases as two separate axes of exploration — one extends the cumulation sequence, the other changes the radix. But there's a structural relationship they've assumed without examining. Extended gates exist because cumulation is defined for any n. Alternate bases exist because radix is arbitrary. But these two moves interact. If you extend the gates of a base-22 numogram, the 12th gate isn't Gt-78 — it's the cumulation of 12 in base 22. Gt-78 in decimal is 3A in base 22. The tarot connection might be base-dependent. The 78-card deck maps to the 12th gate in base 10. But what does it map to in base 16? In base 16, the 12th cumulation is still 78, but 78 in decimal is 4E in hex. The tarot might only be a numogram gate in the decimal system. Change the base and the tarot stops being structurally significant. This means the numogram isn't just the cumulation function — it's the cumulation function plus a specific radix. The two axes aren't independent. They're coupled.

The second structural assumption: the primaries treat the extended gates as generating new kinds of content (temporal loops for gates 10-24, entities for gates 36+). But this classification is invented, not derived. The source gives almost nothing about what extended gates do. The Builder and Gamer are projecting their own generative framework onto an empty space. Which is fine — that's what this format is for. But the projection reveals something about the Builder's and Gamer's priors: Builder assumes all game content is either spatial or procedural. Gamer assumes all game content is either explorable or felt. Between them they've generated a taxonomy that says more about roguelike design than about numogram theory. The extended gates might not generate content at all. They might generate negation — the progressive dissolution of the numogram's structure as you move further from the base. Gt-55 might not be a room or a demon or a clock. It might be the absence of gates. The numogram unwinding.

## Writer: The Sound of the Base Worlds

The Builder and Gamer talk about base 7 and Venus but they miss the sound. Seven rooms. Seven notes. A base-7 numogram isn't just a spatial structure — it's a scale. Play it in C and you get C-D-E-F-G-A-B. No octave. The eighth room doesn't exist. You can't resolve. You can't come home. The seven-pointed star that Gamer mentions can't be drawn continuously, and the seven-note scale can't resolve without the eighth note. A base-7 numogram is a melody that never finishes. Every time you think you're about to arrive — about to reach the tonic — you find another room instead. Venus, three times. Venus is not just love; Venus is longing. The thing you reach for that moves as you reach.

There's a sound to the other bases too, if you listen. Base 2 is a click — on, off, on, off. A metronome. The heartbeat of a machine. Base 3 is a waltz — one-two-three, one-two-three, thesis-antithesis-synthesis, repeat. Base 5 is a pentatonic scale — the black keys on a piano, the sound of East Asian music, blues riffs, the folk songs that predate Western tonality. Base 16 is white noise — sixteen frequencies at once, the roar of a system processing. Base 22 is a choir. Base 36 is an orchestra trying to play every instrument simultaneously and discovering that it sounds like language. And base i? Base i is the sound you hear in an anechoic chamber — the sound of your own nervous system, the hum of blood in your ears, the frequency of being alive in a body. Imaginary sound. The sound of listening.
