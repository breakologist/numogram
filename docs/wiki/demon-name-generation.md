# Demon Name Generation

The CCRU's Pandemonium Matrix demon names were not invented arbitrarily. They were generated through a **combinatorial phonetic system** where each zone carries a syllabic value, and demon names are constructed by combining the sounds of their two zone-indices.

## Discovery

**Amy Ireland** (co-author of *Cute Accelerationism*) discovered the relationship between the numogram and demon name generation. As Nick Land confirms:

> "It turns out that it was actually Amy Ireland who figured out the relationship between the numogram and how to generate the demon's names... showing how the Munamese quasi-phonetic particles map onto the numog." — Nick Land, *Numogram Explained*

## The Method

The system is **combinatorial, not derivational**:

1. Each zone is assigned a **salabic value** (a consonant cluster or phonetic particle)
2. A demon's name combines the particles from its two zones
3. Vowels are added freely to make the result pronounceable
4. The result is then loosely associated with etymological resonances

Land emphasizes: *"It's not very rigorous or strict... it's just like stuff swirling around."*

## Known Examples

### Katak (5::4, Sink)
- Zone 5 contributes a **"k"** sound
- Zone 4 contributes a **"t"** sound
- Combined: **kata** + **k** → *Katak*
- Etymological resonance: "catastrophe," "kata-" (Greek: down, against)

> "There's a C sound on the five zone and some kind of t sound on the four zone... katak so I think... kata on catastrophe, kata kex something like that." — Nick Land

### Oddubb (7::2, Hold)
- Zone 7 contributes a **"dub"** sound
- Zone 2 contributes a **"b"** sound
- Combined: **od** + **dub** + **b** → *Oddubb*
- Note the 7::2 syzygy — both sounds are bilabial/plosive in character

> "We had odub was this 7::2 which is again a dub sound that we just added whatever vowels we need to make that into a... sound." — Nick Land

## Land's Naming Rules

Land mentions having **"rules for what counts as a name for a scissory"** (a scissory is a cutting point or division in the numogram structure). These rules are not fully documented, but from the transcript we can infer:

1. **Two-zone composition** — a name must draw from both zones of the demon's syzygy
2. **Phonetic plausibility** — the result must be pronounceable (vowels can be inserted)
3. **Etymological resonance** — the name should evoke something (catastrophe, dub, etc.)
4. **AQ verification** — the name's AQ value should reduce meaningfully (this is speculative but consistent with numogram practice)

## Application: Generating New Demon Names

Given a syzygy (e.g., 6::3, Warp), the procedure would be:

1. Take the quasiphonic particles of both zones: Zone 6 = **tch**, Zone 3 = **zx**
2. Combine: tch + zx → *tchzx* (unpronounceable)
3. Add vowels: **Tchazax**, **Zextchu**, **Atchizox**
4. Check etymological resonance: "Tchazax" evokes static + buzz = interference pattern
5. Verify AQ: TCHAZAX = ? → reduce to zone → should map back to Warp

This is the **Munamese method** — named after the Nma language of the CCRU's fictional Lemurian mythology.

## Implications for the Djynxxogram

The base-36 Djynxxogram (630 demons) would need a phonetic system for zones 10-35. Two approaches:

1. **Extend the quasiphonic particles** — assign new sounds to zones A-Z based on their digital-root parent
2. **Use the octave hypothesis** — Zone 10 (A) = Surge-prime = same particle as Zone 1 but "higher"

> "I don't even remember what our basic syllables were for this. But... this guy went and he put I think he put the syllables on each zone. Yeah. Yeah. Yeah. That would make sense." — Nick Land

The syllables exist but were never formally published. Reconstructing them is an open research problem.
